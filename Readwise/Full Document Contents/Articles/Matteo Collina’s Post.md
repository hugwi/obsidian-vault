The Human in the Loop: Why Review is the New Bottleneck

Mike Arnaldi wrote a thought-provoking piece called "The Death of Software Development." He argues that AI has fundamentally changed our industry, that 40 years of best practices are now outdated, and that software engineers are more crucial than ever.

He's right about all of that.

But I think he's missing something critical.

My workflow has changed dramatically. When an issue lands in my queue today, my first instinct is to throw it at AI. Security vulnerabilities in Undici. Bugs in Fastify. New features for Platformatic. AI handles the implementation.

But here's the thing: I review every single change. Every behavior modification. Every line that ships.

My ability to ship is no longer limited by how fast I can code. It's limited by my skill to review. And I think that's exactly how it should be.

The role of the programmer who takes a task from Jira, does it, and clocks off for the day? That's gone. AI can do that now, faster and cheaper.

But reviewing and assessing code done by others is what we've done in open source since forever. As a maintainer of [Node.js](http://Node.js?trk=public_post-text), Fastify, Pino, and Undici, and as Chair of the [Node.js](http://Node.js?trk=public_post-text) Technical Steering Committee, I spend most of my time reviewing pull requests from contributors I've never met. I don't write most of the code that ships. I review it, I assess it, I decide if it's good enough.

AI is just another contributor now.

I've also shipped contributions that I didn't fully understand. I regret them fondly. Every maintainer has done this at some point. And every time, it comes back to bite you. The bugs are harder to fix. The behavior is harder to explain. The technical debt compounds.

This is why the human-in-the-loop isn't a limitation to overcome. It's a feature to protect.  

The most critical skill for developers in 2026 isn't prompting. It's judgment.
